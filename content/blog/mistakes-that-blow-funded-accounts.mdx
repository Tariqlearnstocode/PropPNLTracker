Passing a prop firm challenge is hard. Only about <Highlight>4-5%</Highlight> of traders make it through on their first attempt. But here is the part nobody talks about enough: the average funded account lasts roughly <Highlight>three weeks</Highlight> before the trader violates a rule or hits the drawdown limit. Getting funded is not the finish line. It is where the real test begins.

After talking to dozens of funded traders and analyzing community data, the same five mistakes show up over and over again. These are not exotic edge cases. They are predictable patterns that blow accounts with almost mechanical reliability. Here is what they are and how to avoid them.

## Mistake #1: Oversizing After Passing the Challenge

You traded 2 contracts of ES during the evaluation. You were disciplined, patient, and methodical. You passed. Now you have a <Highlight>$150,000</Highlight> funded account and the rules allow up to 15 contracts. On day one, you start trading 5, 8, or 10 contracts because — you reason — you have "proven yourself" and want to maximize your payouts.

This is the most common account killer. The strategy that passed your challenge was calibrated for a specific position size. The risk per trade, the stop loss placement, the mental comfort with drawdowns — all of it was built around 2 contracts. When you triple or quadruple your size overnight, everything breaks.

With 2 contracts on ES, a 10-point adverse move costs you <Highlight>$1,000</Highlight>. With 8 contracts, that same move costs <Highlight>$4,000</Highlight>. On a $150,000 account with a <Highlight>$4,500</Highlight> trailing drawdown, a single bad trade at 8 contracts can consume nearly your entire buffer. The math that felt safe at 2 contracts becomes catastrophic at 8.

### How to Avoid It

Trade the funded account at the same size you used to pass the challenge for at least the first <Highlight>2-4 weeks</Highlight>. If you traded 2 contracts during the eval, trade 2 contracts funded. Only increase size after you have built a profit buffer that can absorb the larger drawdowns. A reasonable rule: increase by 1 contract for every <Highlight>$2,000</Highlight> in profit above your starting balance.

## Mistake #2: Ignoring Trailing Drawdown Mechanics

Trailing drawdown is the rule that kills the most funded accounts at firms like Topstep and Apex, and it works differently than most traders intuitively expect.

Here is a scenario. You have a <Highlight>$150,000</Highlight> account with a <Highlight>$4,500</Highlight> trailing drawdown. Your floor starts at $145,500. On Monday, you have a great day — your account peaks at <Highlight>$154,200</Highlight> in open equity during a trade. Even if you close the trade at $152,000, your drawdown floor has permanently moved up to <Highlight>$149,700</Highlight> (the peak of $154,200 minus $4,500). Your effective cushion from your current balance of $152,000 is now only <Highlight>$2,300</Highlight>, not $4,500.

Many traders do not realize the drawdown trails on **open equity**, not closed P&L. An unrealized gain during an intraday spike can permanently shrink your buffer. You can have a winning day and still lose half your available drawdown.

### How to Avoid It

- Know exactly how your firm calculates trailing drawdown — open equity vs. closed P&L, intraday vs. end of day.
- Use a tracking tool or write down your current drawdown floor after every session.
- Consider scaling out of winners rather than letting the full position ride, since the trailing drawdown penalizes unrealized peaks.
- Be especially careful in the first few days of funded trading when your buffer is smallest.

## Mistake #3: Revenge Trading After a Red Day

You lose <Highlight>$1,200</Highlight> in the morning session. Your drawdown limit feels uncomfortably close. Instead of stopping for the day, you tell yourself "I just need one good trade to get back to even." You re-enter the market, usually with larger size or looser stops, trying to recover the loss. The result, more often than not, is a second loss that compounds the first. Now you are down <Highlight>$2,500</Highlight> and your funded account is on life support.

Revenge trading is a well-documented behavioral pattern. After a loss, the brain experiences it as a threat, triggering the amygdala and shifting you from analytical thinking to emotional reaction. Your ability to read the market objectively is genuinely impaired. This is not a willpower problem — it is neurochemistry.

### How to Avoid It

- Set a **daily loss limit** before the session starts. When you hit it, you are done for the day. No exceptions.
- A reasonable daily loss limit is <Highlight>30-40%</Highlight> of your trailing drawdown. On a $4,500 drawdown, that means stopping at $1,350-$1,800 in losses.
- Physically step away from the screen. Close the platform. The market will be there tomorrow.
- If you find yourself thinking "I just need one more trade," that is the signal to stop immediately.

## Mistake #4: Not Adjusting for News Events

FOMC announcements, CPI releases, Non-Farm Payrolls, GDP reports — these scheduled news events create massive volatility spikes that can move ES <Highlight>30-50 points</Highlight> in minutes. Many prop firms have specific rules about trading during news events (some ban it outright; others restrict position sizes), but even at firms that allow it, news trading in a funded account is disproportionately risky.

The problem is asymmetric. On your best news trade, you might make <Highlight>$2,000</Highlight>. On your worst, you might get slipped through your stop and lose <Highlight>$3,000-$5,000</Highlight> in seconds. When you only have $4,500 of drawdown room, that single slip can end the account. Professional prop firms can absorb these swings. Funded traders with tight drawdown limits cannot.

### How to Avoid It

- Bookmark an economic calendar (ForexFactory or TradingEconomics) and check it every morning before trading.
- For high-impact events (FOMC, CPI, NFP), either sit out entirely or reduce your position size to <Highlight>25%</Highlight> of normal.
- Close all open positions at least <Highlight>15 minutes</Highlight> before a scheduled announcement.
- If your firm bans news trading, know the exact blackout windows. A violation can cost you the account even if the trade was profitable.

## Mistake #5: Treating Funded Money Like It's Not Real

This is the most insidious mistake because it operates subconsciously. The funded account is not "your" money. It is the firm's capital (or a simulated version of it). This psychological distance makes traders take risks they would never take with their own savings.

Researchers call this the **house money effect**. In casino studies, gamblers who are playing with winnings (rather than their original stake) consistently make larger and riskier bets. The same dynamic plays out in funded accounts. "It's not my money, so what do I have to lose?" What you have to lose is the <Highlight>$500-$2,000</Highlight> you spent getting funded, plus the months of work it took to pass the challenge, plus the future payouts you would have earned from a properly managed account.

### How to Avoid It

Reframe the funded account as **your business asset**. Calculate the total cost of getting funded: challenge fees, resets, data feeds, time invested. That is what you have at stake. If you spent $1,500 getting funded and the account can generate $2,000-$5,000 per month in payouts, blowing it is not a zero-cost event. It is a $1,500 loss plus the opportunity cost of months of future income.

Some traders find it helpful to write the total cost of getting funded on a sticky note and put it on their monitor. When temptation hits to take an impulsive trade, that number serves as a concrete reminder of what is actually at risk.

## The Common Thread

All five mistakes share a root cause: **the rules of funded trading are different from the challenge**, and most traders do not adjust. In the challenge, the goal is to hit a profit target. In the funded account, the goal flips — it becomes about protecting the account and extracting consistent payouts over time. The traders who survive are the ones who make that mental shift on day one.

> The challenge tests whether you can make money. The funded account tests whether you can keep it.
