You made <Highlight>$5,000</Highlight> in profit on your funded account. How much of that do you actually keep? The answer depends on your profit split — and it is not as straightforward as it sounds. Profit splits are the mechanism prop firms use to divide trading profits between the firm and the trader. Most traders know the headline number (80/20, 90/10) but miss the details that determine their actual take-home.

## What a Profit Split Is

A profit split is the percentage of your funded account's trading profit that goes to you versus the firm. An <Highlight>80/20</Highlight> split means you keep 80% of the profit and the firm takes 20%. A <Highlight>90/10</Highlight> split means you keep 90%.

The split applies to your **gross trading profit** — the gains in your funded account above the starting balance. If your account started at $100,000 and you grew it to $105,000, your gross profit is $5,000. At an 80/20 split, the firm pays you <Highlight>$4,000</Highlight> and keeps $1,000.

This sounds simple, but a few nuances change the real math significantly.

## Common Split Structures Across Firms

Profit splits vary by firm and often change over time as you prove consistency:

- **FTMO:** Starts at <Highlight>80/20</Highlight>. After meeting their scaling plan requirements (10% profit over four months with no violations), you move to <Highlight>90/10</Highlight>. The 90/10 split is the maximum.
- **Topstep:** Starts at <Highlight>80/20</Highlight> for your first <Highlight>$10,000</Highlight> in total payouts. After $10K in cumulative payouts, you move to <Highlight>90/10</Highlight> permanently.
- **Apex Trader Funding:** Split varies depending on promotions and account type, typically ranging from <Highlight>75/25</Highlight> to <Highlight>90/10</Highlight>. Their promotional accounts sometimes start at lower splits.
- **The5ers:** Starts at <Highlight>80/20</Highlight> and scales up to <Highlight>100/0</Highlight> (you keep everything) at higher tier levels. Reaching 100/0 requires significant milestones and account growth.

The trend across the industry is toward higher trader splits. Competition has pushed most reputable firms to offer 80/20 as a floor, with a path to 90/10. Firms advertising 90/10 or 100/0 from day one usually compensate with higher challenge fees or stricter rules.

## How Scaling Plans Change Your Split

Most firms offer a scaling plan — a structured path to larger accounts and better splits. The idea is that as you prove you can manage the account responsibly, you earn more favorable terms. Here is how scaling works at FTMO as a concrete example:

- Start with a $100,000 account at 80/20
- After 4 months of profitable trading (minimum 10% profit), your account scales to <Highlight>$125,000</Highlight> and your split moves to <Highlight>90/10</Highlight>
- Continue hitting milestones and the account can grow to <Highlight>$200,000</Highlight>, <Highlight>$400,000</Highlight>, and eventually up to <Highlight>$2,000,000</Highlight>

The scaling plan means your effective split improves over time — but only if you maintain consistency. One violation can reset your scaling progress, depending on the firm's rules.

## Why the Split Percentage Alone Does Not Tell the Whole Story

Here is where most comparisons go wrong. Traders look at splits in isolation — "Firm A offers 90/10 and Firm B offers 80/20, so Firm A is obviously better." But the split is only one variable. The total cost structure determines your real take-home.

Consider two firms:

- **Firm A:** 90/10 split, but the challenge costs $600, resets are $150, and the drawdown rules are tight (leading to more resets).
- **Firm B:** 80/20 split, but the challenge costs $250, resets are $80, and the drawdown rules are more forgiving.

If Firm A requires 4 resets to pass and Firm B requires 1 reset, the total cost to get funded at Firm A is <Highlight>$1,200</Highlight> ($600 + 4 x $150) versus <Highlight>$330</Highlight> ($250 + 1 x $80) at Firm B. The $870 difference in getting-funded cost offsets many months of the better split.

> A 90/10 split with $1,200 in sunk costs is worse than an 80/20 split with $330 in sunk costs until you have earned enough in payouts for the split difference to make up the gap. Do the math for your specific situation.

## How Fees Reduce Your Effective Split

Your effective split is not the number on the firm's marketing page. It is what you actually keep after all costs are deducted. Here is a realistic example:

A trader with a $100K account at 80/20 earns <Highlight>$5,000</Highlight> in gross profit. Their payout is $4,000. But to reach this point, they spent:

- Challenge fee: <Highlight>$350</Highlight>
- Two resets: <Highlight>$200</Highlight>
- Two months of data feeds: <Highlight>$260</Highlight>
- Activation fee: <Highlight>$130</Highlight>

Total expenses: <Highlight>$940</Highlight>. Net take-home from that $5,000 in trading profit: **$3,060**. That is an effective split of <Highlight>61/39</Highlight> — not 80/20. The firm's 20% share was $1,000, but your fees added another $940 in costs. Your actual take-home rate on the gross profit was 61.2%.

The effective split improves over time as your initial costs are amortized across more payouts. By the third or fourth payout, the per-payout cost drops significantly. But that first payout almost always delivers a much lower effective split than the headline number.

## Calculating Your Real Numbers

To know your actual effective split, you need two numbers: total money deposited into your bank from a specific firm, and total money debited from your bank to that firm (or its processor). The ratio between these tells you what you are really keeping.

Tracking this manually means combing through bank statements, matching Riseworks and Wise deposits to the right firms, and adding up every challenge fee and reset that may have been charged months ago. It is doable for one firm. It becomes unsustainable across three or four.
